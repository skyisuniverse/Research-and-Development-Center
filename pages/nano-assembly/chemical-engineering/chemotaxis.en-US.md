# Chemotaxis

[Chemotaxis (Grokipedia) ](https://grokipedia.com/page/Chemotaxis)

[Chemotaxis (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemotaxis)

> Chemotaxis (from chemo- + taxis) is the movement of an organism or entity in response to a chemical stimulus.

> Chemotaxis is the movement of organisms or cells, such as bacteria or leukocytes, toward (attractants) or away from (repellents) specific chemical signals in their environment. This crucial biological process guides movement toward nutrients or away from toxins, and plays key roles in immune response and bacterial colonization.

## Key Aspects of Chemotaxis:

- Mechanism in Bacteria: Bacteria use flagella to swim toward attractants (like sugars) and away from toxins. They use a "run and tumble" movement, which is a purposeful random walk directed by sensing chemical concentrations.

- Signaling Pathway: Bacteria sense substances using receptors such as Methyl-accepting Chemotaxis Proteins (MCPs), which initiate a phosphorylation cascade (involving CheA and CheY proteins) that changes flagellar rotation

### Methyl-accepting Chemotaxis Proteins (MCPs)

[Methyl-accepting chemotaxis proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl-accepting_chemotaxis_proteins)

Methyl-accepting Chemotaxis Proteins (MCPs) are specialized transmembrane receptors in bacteria and archaea that detect environmental chemical gradients (attractants or repellents) to guide motility. As the core sensing element, they form large hexagonal arrays at cellular poles, signaling through conformational changes that regulate CheA kinase activity to control flagellar motor rotation.

---

CheA and CheY are core proteins in bacterial chemotaxis, forming a two-component signaling system that controls flagellar rotation and direction. CheA is a histidine kinase that autophosphorylates, while CheY is a response regulator that receives the phosphate, causing "tumbling" to change direction, or "swimming" when dephosphorylated.
